One process, many observations

Every stream is the same infections, delayed and rescaled

We rarely watch an outbreak through a single window. We usually see confirmed cases, deaths, and increasingly a wastewater signal — all at once. It is tempting to treat them as three separate epidemics, because they peak at different times and sit at wildly different levels. The whole point of a joint model is that they are not separate: there is one latent infection trajectory I(t), and each stream is that same trajectory passed through its own lens.

A lens has two parts. A delay distribution p(s) — how long after infection this stream tends to record the event — which shifts and smears the curve. And a scaling — what fraction of infections this stream captures, and on what scale — which moves the level up or down without changing the shape:

stream(t) = scaling × Σs I(t − s) · p(s)

The shared infections are generated by the renewal equation from the reproduction number and the generation time, exactly as in the earlier sessions:

I(t) = R(t) · Σs≥1 I(t − s) · g(s)

Takeaways

  • Cases, deaths and wastewater are one shared infection trajectory seen through three lenses — a per-stream delay and a per-stream scaling — not three separate epidemics.
  • Scaling sets the level of a stream; it does not change the shape. This is why the absolute level of infections is hard to identify: any stream is happy with double the infections and half the scaling.
  • Delay shifts a stream later and smears it. Deaths peak latest because their infection-to-death delay is longest.
  • A joint (parallel) model uses this: every stream informs the same latent infections, so timely streams sharpen the recent trajectory while stable streams anchor the level — see the session.
  • When streams cannot be reconciled by one shared trajectory, that is genuine conflict, and it signals that a delay or scaling assumption is wrong for at least one stream.
