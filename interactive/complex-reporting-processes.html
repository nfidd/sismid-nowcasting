Day-of-week reporting effects

A surveillance system does not process reports evenly across the week: few land at the weekend, and Monday catches up. So the observed count series develops a weekly sawtooth that is an artefact of reporting, not of transmission. The underlying epidemic is smooth. The weekly wobble is about reporting, not transmission

A smooth epidemic can look jagged simply because of when reports are processed

Imagine the true number of events each day — the underlying expected counts — follows a smooth epidemic curve. Transmission does not care what day of the week it is. But the reporting system does: staff, laboratories and data pipelines run at different intensities on different weekdays, so fewer reports are entered at weekends and a backlog is cleared early the following week.

Write the day of week of date t as t mod 7, with day 0 a Monday (matching the course simulation). Each weekday k carries a multiplicative reporting weight wk. The count we actually see is the smooth signal scaled by that day's weight, so weekends are pushed down and Mondays pulled up:

  • underlyingt — the smooth expected counts (the epidemic itself).
  • observedt = underlyingt × w(t mod 7) — the same signal seen through a weekly reporting filter.

The weights are normalised to average one across the week, so they only redistribute reports between weekdays rather than change the overall level. That means the observed series wobbles around the smooth curve, and the wobble is a pure reporting artefact.

A drop in the last two days of a raw curve is very often just the weekend, not a real downturn. The pattern repeats every seven days — that regularity is the giveaway that it is the calendar, not the epidemic, talking.

Set the weekly weights to make the sawtooth deeper or shallower; the adjusted series recovers the smooth curve because dividing by the same normalised weight undoes the scaling. Turn on Poisson noise to see the small residual scatter that no day-of-week correction can remove.

With the weights flat (all equal), the normalised pattern is all ones, so observed, adjusted and underlying coincide — the correction is the identity. Push the weekend weights down and the observed sawtooth deepens, yet the adjusted series barely moves: the reporting pattern has been divided out.

The correction is one division per day

Normalise the weekly weights, multiply to distort, divide to recover

Let w0, …, w6 be the raw weekday weights (Mon…Sun) and w̄ their mean. Normalising by the mean gives a pattern that averages to one over the week, so it only moves reports between weekdays:

w̃k = wkw̄ ,    17 Σk=06 w̃k = 1

The observed count is the smooth expectation scaled by that day's normalised weight (optionally with Poisson reporting noise on top). The day-of-week-adjusted series just divides it straight back out:

observedt = underlyingt · w̃(t mod 7)   →   adjustedt = observedtw̃(t mod 7) = underlyingt

Because the same normalised weight appears in the multiplication and the division, the adjustment recovers the underlying signal exactly (up to reporting noise). If every weight is equal then each w̃k = 1 and both steps are the identity, which is why a system with no day-of-week effect is left untouched.

Why normalise? Without normalising, dividing by the raw weights would also rescale the overall level of the curve. Normalising to mean one separates the two questions: the weekly shape (what the correction removes) from the overall reporting level (which a nowcasting model handles elsewhere). This mirrors how the course model uses a report-date day-of-week term keyed on the report date t mod 7.

Takeaways

  • A regular weekly sawtooth in a count series is usually a reporting artefact: reports are processed unevenly across weekdays, not created unevenly.
  • Model it as a multiplicative weekday weight w(t mod 7) on the report date, with day 0 a Monday to match the course simulation.
  • Normalise the weights to average one so they redistribute reports between weekdays without changing the overall level.
  • The day-of-week adjustment divides the pattern back out, recovering the smooth signal exactly when the true weights are known; flat weights leave the series unchanged.
  • A correction cannot invent information: Poisson noise stays, and the deep weekend troughs are where the adjusted counts are least certain because so few reports were seen.
  • In practice you do not know the true weights — you estimate them jointly with the nowcast, which is exactly what a report-date day-of-week term in epinowcast does.
